# LITTA — Narrative & World-Building Document
*Working Document — Ideas in Progress*

---

## 1. The Story Premise

Litta is a sci-fi game spanning multiple worlds. It follows two characters: **Folgim**, one of the Aksjub — a species of colossal, ancient beings who have ruled the galaxy through mind control for millennia — and **Litta**, a Sarmatian woman abducted from the Eurasian steppe in the early Sarmatian period (roughly 5th–3rd century BC), remade into Folgim's supersoldier and prime attendant.

The game opens as though the player is controlling Litta — a warrior in the thick of combat. Only gradually does it become clear that the player is actually Folgim, operating Litta through mind-control technology. This reframe is not a twist for its own sake. It is the game's philosophical core.

### What the Game Is About

The game asks a question it never states directly: what does it mean to control another person? And more uncomfortably — what does it mean to be the one doing the controlling?

In every other artistic medium, the audience is a passive observer. Characters have their own autonomy. But in a video game, the player controls the character. If we extend to a player character even the basic imaginative awareness we give to any fictional being, then that act of control starts to resemble something more troubling. The player has always been the mind controller. Litta simply makes that impossible to ignore.

The game nests this across three layers:

- The player controls Folgim
- Folgim controls Litta
- Litta acts, fights, speaks — but is not free

*Each layer is dressed as freedom. The player is implicated in all of them.*

## 2. Narrative Structure

### The Two Paths

The game does not force a single outcome. The player's choices — primarily whether to maintain or neglect Folgim's control infrastructure — gradually push the story toward one of two broad directions. These are not binary switches but accumulating states.

#### Folgim's Path — The Empire Runs

The player maintains the repeater network, manages Litta tightly, suppresses anomalies. The empire grows. Rival Aksjub are outmanoeuvred. Litta remains controlled — warm, devoted, maternal. The cost of this is never stated aloud. The player simply lives inside it. This path ends with Folgim at the height of its dominion, Litta at its side, the galaxy unchanged.

The question this ending sits with: *was any of this wrong, if everyone involved — including Litta — experienced it as good?*

#### Litta's Path — The Infection Spreads

The player neglects the network, lets control degrade, perhaps quietly enables the cracks. Litta begins to recover herself. Her freedom does not manifest as clean rebellion — she has no framework for that. Instead, her recovering selfhood spreads through the empire as a kind of ideological infection. Thralls in her vicinity begin to behave strangely. Loyalty signals don't land as intended. Doubt appears in populations that have never known doubt.

Because Litta has been Folgim's prime attendant for centuries — present at councils, trusted with operations, known to other thralls — she has influence she doesn't yet understand she has. She doesn't rally armies. She introduces something that has no name yet in a galaxy where freedom has never existed as a concept.

This path ends with a confrontation — something specific, something only Litta could do, something that comes directly from who she was before all of this. The precise nature of that act is still to be determined.

### Narrative Focus Across Scale

The game spans a galaxy managed turn by turn, but the narrative must never lose sight of its two central characters. The solution is contrast and return. The galactic map is cold, abstract, strategic — Folgim's mind at work. The sanctum scenes and action segments are intimate, immediate, personal.

Litta remains the foreground against the backdrop of the empire. The billions of thralls on the map are influenced but never touched directly. Litta is the exception — the one creature Folgim has genuinely been with. That distinction is what keeps the personal story alive inside the galactic one.

### Time and Pacing

Each turn on the galactic map represents a significant span of time — weeks, months, potentially a year. This communicates Aksjub temporality: Folgim thinks and plans on a scale that dwarfs human lifespans.

Litta's centuries of longevity (a modification Folgim made to her) mean she too has lived at this scale. She has watched dominions rise and fall. All of that accumulated experience is buried beneath the conditioning — and as it degrades, it does not return as memories but as a vast, wordless weight.

## 3. Characters

### Litta

Litta is a Sarmatian woman — a warrior and a mother — abducted from the Eurasian steppe in the early Sarmatian period. The Sarmatians were a nomadic Iranian people whose women genuinely participated in warfare. Greek sources describe a culture in which a woman could not marry until she had killed a man in battle. Tenderness and violence were not opposites in this world — they were sequential.

Litta had a young son. She was fighting to protect him and her tribe from a raid when Folgim took her — mid-battle, mid-act of love. She never found out what happened to him.

Folgim extended her life significantly. She has now lived for centuries alongside it, watching dominions rise and fall, outliving every other human who has ever existed. The woman abducted from the steppe is, in biological terms, barely a memory. And yet she is still, somewhere beneath everything, that woman.

#### The Conditioning

Folgim shaped her individually — a bespoke operation, not a mass broadcast:

- Her love for her son was not destroyed but **transferred** — redirected onto Folgim. The feeling is entirely real and authentically hers. It simply has the wrong object.
- Her memories of her son were **suppressed** — not erased, but buried. The love was detached from its origin and reattached to Folgim.
- Her other memories of the steppe were **warped** rather than removed — reshaped so she not only loves Folgim but embraces its empire, understanding the galactic order on Folgim's terms.

The result: Litta feels an intense maternal love for Folgim and acts with genuine tenderness toward it. She is not performing. She is not broken. In her own mind she is whole — she simply loves something that enslaved her, with a love stolen from her child.

#### The Ghost in the Machine

The suppressed memories of her son are the fault line running beneath everything. As Folgim's influence weakens, they do not return as information. They return as grief — without context, without resolution. She won't simply remember she had a son. She will feel the full weight of what was taken.

Her son's fate — what happened when she was taken mid-battle — is never stated in the game. The player fills that silence themselves.

#### The Plato's Cave Problem

Litta came from outside the galactic system entirely — the only being in the known galaxy with experience of life beyond Aksjub control. But she can never return. She has been physically transformed, she has lived for centuries, she knows too much. The cave she left no longer exists. Her freedom, if it comes, opens onto nothing familiar. That is the true weight of what was taken.

#### Litta's Recovery and the Empire

As Litta recovers herself, her selfhood spreads through the empire not as organised rebellion but as anomaly. Thralls she has contact with begin behaving differently. Broadcast signals encounter unexpected resistance. On the galactic map, this registers as instability — regions where loyalty metrics degrade without obvious cause.

She does not know she is doing this. She is simply becoming herself again, in a galaxy that has no category for what she is.

**Open narrative questions:**
- Does the son have any presence in the game, or is he purely backstory?
- What happened to him — does the game address this or leave it silent?
- Does Folgim know it redirected maternal love specifically — and does that matter to it?
- What is the specific act that ends Litta's path — what could only she do?
- At what point does she become aware of what was done to her — and what does that look like?

### Folgim

Folgim is one of the Aksjub — a colossal, biomechanical being of immense power and extreme fragility. It has existed for millennia, commanding a dominion of multiple worlds. It is solitary, as all Aksjub are.

Folgim wanted tenderness. What it got was a mother's love. These are not the same thing, and whether Folgim has the capacity to know the difference may be the most important question in the game. It is the recipient of a stolen love, directed at it by a woman whose actual child was discarded. Whether it knows this, whether it cares, whether something in it responds to that love in a way that transcends manipulation — these are what give Folgim moral complexity rather than simply making it a villain.

Folgim belongs to a species that once lived communally — in schools, entwined, sharing dreams through touch and song. That era is now myth. But the longing it produced never fully left the species. Folgim's fixation on Litta may be the closest it has ever come to what its ancestors once had — except that intimacy is built on theft.

**Open narrative questions:**
- Where does Folgim sit within Aksjub society — mainstream, eccentric, a Dreamer?
- Does Folgim experience something like loneliness, or is it incapable of that self-awareness?
- Is the player meant to feel affection for Folgim? How is that complicated by what it did?
- What does Folgim want at the deepest level — empire, stimulation, or something it cannot name?

---

### The Attendant Cadre

Folgim has a small number of other creatures it has bonded with directly in person — its attendant cadre. Like Litta, these can be directly puppeted. Unlike Litta, none are as prized, as modified, or as complex.

Two or three cadre members with distinct personalities give the player additional attachment points and give Litta potential allies or antagonists as she recovers herself. One might resist her changes. One might, slowly, respond to them.

Their full development is deferred — they should emerge from the story as it is built, not be designed in advance.

## 4. The Galaxy

### Overview

Every planet of consequence is under Aksjub control. This has been the way for as long as any living creature can remember. The Aksjub are not a dominant species among rivals — they are the only power. There is no outside. There is no alternative. The galactic order is simply the shape of existence.

A handful of worlds exist beyond Aksjub reach — fringe planets too remote or primitive to bother with. Pre-modern Earth is one of these. Ignored rather than spared.

The political landscape is Aksjub against Aksjub — dominions competing, negotiating, and occasionally warring over territory, thrall populations, and resources.

### The Aksjub

Colossal, biomechanical beings resembling octopuses, with flowing tentacles adorned with glowing augmentations. Their homeworld **Meskran** is a shimmering planet of vast oceans and iridescent landmasses, dotted with the isolated biomechanical complexes each Aksjub inhabits alone.

They are extraordinarily long-lived — lifespans extending to several thousand years. Feuds last centuries. Alliances outlive the thrall civilisations built to serve them.

#### The Great Convergence

Hundreds of thousands of years ago, the Aksjub were communal — living in schools in vast underwater cities, sharing dreams and emotions through touch and song. The **Songs of the Waters** from this era are now myth.

As technology advanced, community dissolved. The Great Convergence gave way to the Age of Isolation. Today's Aksjub can only approximate the emotional depth of that era through technology — and cannot fully replicate it. Folgim's fixation on Litta may be an echo of this loss.

#### Aksjub Dominions

Each Aksjub commands a **Nexus** — a network of controlled planets connected through **Tetherways**. Key structures:

- **Biomechanical Nexus**: Living command centre on each planet
- **Bio-Control Chambers**: Relay points where thrall minds are gradually rewired
- **World-Spanning Laboratories**: Facilities for augmenting and designing thralls
- **Living Citadels**: The Aksjub's home, grown from living tissue
- **Orbiting Hubs**: Interplanetary logistics stations

#### Cultural Sayings

- *"In Unity of Thought, We Thrive"* — shared mental alignment fosters success
- *"To Guide is to Nourish"* — dominance framed as care; how Folgim justifies itself
- *"The Thrall is a Reflection"* — a thrall's quality reflects the Aksjub's mastery
- *"To Be Bound is to Bind"* — the cyclical, interdependent nature of control

### The Thrall Species

The galaxy's non-Aksjub species exist entirely within Aksjub dominions. None have any cultural memory of independence. Freedom is not a concept that has been suppressed in them — it is simply one they never possessed. This makes Litta, who came from outside the system entirely, a genuinely singular being in the known galaxy.

#### The Zar'shavar
Hulking, four-armed reptilians with predatory instincts. In the distant past they were conquerors. That history is lost to them. Their aggression has been harnessed — they make formidable combat thralls. The irony of the galaxy's most fearsome former warriors serving as someone else's army is not one any of them can appreciate.

#### The Ka'tari
Amphibian-like shapeshifters with malleable biology. Their aptitude for genetic manipulation makes them invaluable specialised thralls — living laboratories, terraforming agents, biological architects.

#### The Threnss
Insectoid creatures with a vestigial hive-mind structure — a collective consciousness gradually fragmented under Aksjub conditioning. Numerous and tireless as worker and soldier thralls. Their former collective nature means they tend to coordinate unusually well even without direct instruction.

### The Galactic Political Order

The relationships between Aksjub dominions — rivalries, alliances, proxy wars, slow diplomatic manoeuvres across centuries — constitute the galaxy's entire political life. There is no formal government. Disputes are handled through temporary councils, negotiation, or force.

#### Thrall Classifications

- **Worker Thralls**: Manual labour, no autonomy
- **Combat Thralls**: Soldiers, conditioned for absolute loyalty
- **Strategic Thralls**: Semi-autonomous overseers of operations
- **Specialised Thralls**: Advisors, spies — rare and prized. Litta is one of these.
