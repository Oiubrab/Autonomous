# AUTONOMOUS — Game Mechanics Document
*Working Document — Ideas in Progress*

---

## 1. The Central Mechanic Premise

Every system in Litta exists to make the player feel something about control, agency, and complicity that cannot be communicated through story alone. The mechanics are not decorative — they are the argument.

The core premise: the player believes they are controlling Litta. They are not. They are controlling Folgim — an ancient alien being — who is in turn controlling Litta through mind-control technology. The player is one layer removed from the character they think they inhabit. This is discovered gradually, not announced.

---

## 2. The Reframe

The game opens in action. The player is placed into combat, apparently as Litta. Controls, camera, and framing suggest a conventional third-person action game. The player bonds with Litta — her movement, her fighting, her voice. They feel like her.

The reframe — the revelation that the player is Folgim, operating Litta through mind control — should land as both a narrative and mechanical shock. After this moment, all prior gameplay takes on new meaning retroactively. Every input the player made was Folgim's will, not Litta's.

### The Death Screen Inversion

When Litta is killed, the game does not end. Folgim still exists; Litta is replaceable. When Folgim is killed or incapacitated, the game ends — the *you died* screen appears. This tells the story without words. The player will feel the discomfort of Litta's life being the expendable one, especially after the opening investment.

### Control Mapping as Storytelling

In dialogue and sanctum scenes, controls are explicitly mapped to Folgim even before the full reframe — the player chooses what Folgim says, and Litta responds. This asymmetry is a quiet tell planted for attentive players. After the reframe, the same mapping is understood differently.

## 3. The Two Systems of Mind Control

### Direct Control — Body Puppeting

An Aksjub can reach directly into a creature's mind and take full physical control of its body. This is exhausting and can only be sustained on one creature at a time. Critically, it **requires a prior in-person bond** — an Aksjub can only directly puppet a creature it has physically been with.

This is what the player does when they move Litta. Every input is Folgim's reach into her body. Litta is not the player character. She is the instrument.

### Broadcast Control — Thought and Emotion Manipulation

Through the network of repeater stations seeded across Folgim's dominion, it can broadcast signals of loyalty, devotion, and emotional state to billions of creatures across many worlds simultaneously. These creatures are not puppets — they move freely and make decisions — but within an emotional landscape Folgim has engineered. They serve willingly because they feel they want to. This is how the empire functions.

### The In-Person Bond Requirement

Aksjub can only directly puppet creatures they have bonded with in person. This is what makes Litta — and the attendant cadre — mechanically unique within the empire. The billions of thralls on the galactic map can be influenced but never directly controlled. Only the cadre can be puppeted. Litta above all others.

This also makes Folgim's fragility narratively significant: it leaves its citadel almost never, meaning direct bonds are rare, hard-won, and precious.

## 4. Game Structure — Three Tiers

The game operates across three tiers of zoom, each with distinct mechanics. Moving between them is the central rhythm of play.

### Tier 1 — The Galactic Map (Turn-Based)

The top-level view is a turn-based map of stars, systems, and planets. Each turn represents a significant span of time — weeks, months, potentially a year — communicating the Aksjub scale of thought and planning. This is Folgim's mind: cold, vast, strategic.

On this map, the player can:
- View the status of planets, systems, and populations within the dominion
- Send mind-control broadcasts to planets as a whole, or to specific settlements and regions
- Monitor the health of the repeater network — the infrastructure of empire
- Engage with rival Aksjub through diplomacy, proxy pressure, or conflict
- Observe Litta's current location and deployment status

The galactic map also registers the effects of Litta's recovering selfhood as **systemic anomalies** — regions of unexpected loyalty degradation, populations behaving outside modelled parameters. These are the map-level consequences of the narrative path the player is on.

### Tier 2 — Planetary Zoom (Real-Time with LLM Broadcasting)

In moments of crisis or active engagement, the player zooms into a single planet and interacts with groups of creatures more directly. This tier operates in a softer real-time — not frantic, but alive.

#### The Thought-Broadcasting Mechanic

This is the most novel mechanic in the game. Rather than selecting units and issuing commands, the player **broadcasts thoughts, feelings, and directives** into the collective emotional landscape of the creatures on a planet. The interface should feel less like a command line and more like a pulse — a transmission.

The player types input such as:
- *"The eastern border fills you with unease — protect it"*
- *"The harvest world must feel urgency — abundance is not guaranteed"*

An LLM interprets this input and translates it into modifications to a **behaviour configuration**: weighted parameters governing creature priorities, emotional states, aggression levels, and resource focus.

The creatures run on autonomous AI reading from that config. They are not receiving orders. They are living inside an emotional landscape the player has shaped. **The player never commands — they author the environment that produces behaviour.**

#### Imprecision as a Feature

The LLM interpretation introduces inherent imprecision — thematically correct for a system based on manipulation rather than command. The gap between intent and outcome is part of the mechanic. Inputs may affect more of the population than intended. Different species respond differently:

- **Zar'shavar** combat thralls may respond to fear and urgency
- **Threnss** workers may need comfort and purpose
- **Ka'tari** specialists may require more nuanced framing

Learning this vocabulary is part of mastering the strategy layer.

### Tier 3 — Action Segments (Direct Control via Litta)

When Litta is on-site during a crisis, the player can zoom in further and take direct control of her in real-time action. This is the **only tier where the player has full, immediate, physical control** of anything in the game. Everything else is nudged and influenced. Litta alone is owned.

This contrast is intentional. The difference between ruling through suggestion and controlling through direct puppetry is felt in the shift between tiers.

The action segments are where the player is most likely to bond with Litta — her speed, her combat, her survival. This is the illusion. The reframe hits harder the more investment the player has made here.

## 5. The Control Degradation System

The repeater network is the infrastructure of Folgim's control over Litta specifically (distinct from the broader empire). Missions task the player with maintaining and expanding it. If missions are failed — intentionally or otherwise — the network degrades and Folgim's grip on Litta weakens.

### Degradation Stages — Action Layer

In action segments, degradation manifests physically:

- **Early**: Slight input lag — Litta hesitates a half-second before responding
- **Developing**: She occasionally moves in a direction the player did not input
- **Heavy**: She stops, looks around, sits down — ignores commands for stretches
- **Full breakdown**: Litta acts entirely on her own. The player watches.

### Degradation Stages — Map Layer

On the galactic map, degradation shows as systemic anomaly — loyalty metrics degrading in populations connected to Litta, broadcast signals not landing as expected, regions exhibiting unusual autonomous behaviour.

### The Inverted Reward Structure

This mechanic inverts conventional game logic. Failing a mission doesn't just cost resources — it **gives Litta something: herself**. The punishment for failure is that the puppet becomes a person. The player must sit with experiencing Litta's freedom as a loss. The game never tells them how to feel about it.

---

## 6. Sanctum Scenes

Intimate, dialogue-driven scenes in Folgim's inner sanctum, with Litta present. All dialogue choices are mapped to Folgim. The player chooses what the captor says. Litta responds warmly — she has been conditioned to. These are the game's quietest and most unsettling segments.

The player, as Folgim, is the recipient of a stolen maternal love. Choosing what Folgim says to Litta is choosing how the captor speaks to someone who loves it with a devotion that was never meant for it.

As the network degrades, sanctum scenes grow more complex. Litta begins to have responses Folgim did not anticipate. Small. Tentative. But hers.

---

## 7. Narrative Endpoints

Player choices accumulate into one of two broad narrative directions. These are not binary switches but emergent states.

### Folgim's Path

Network maintained, anomalies suppressed, Litta controlled. The empire grows. The game ends with Folgim at the height of its dominion, Litta at its side, the galaxy unchanged. The question this ending sits with is never asked aloud.

### Litta's Path

Network degraded, anomalies ignored or enabled. Litta recovers herself. Her selfhood spreads through the empire as ideological infection — registered on the galactic map as instability. Eventually a confrontation occurs. Its nature is still to be determined, but it should be something only Litta could do — something that comes directly from who she was before all of this.

Whether this constitutes victory, loss, or something else is deliberately left open.

## 8. Production Notes

### Tools
- **Engine**: Godot
- **Development assistance**: Claude Code
- **LLM integration**: Anthropic API (for thought-broadcasting mechanic in tier 2)
- **Personal project** — scope should be managed carefully

### Scope Guidance

The concept is strong enough to work at modest scope. A vertical slice covering one example of each tier — a galactic map turn cycle, a planetary broadcast sequence, one action segment with Litta, and one sanctum scene — would be enough to test whether the whole thing holds together.

Build the opening action segment first. Then build the reframe moment. That single transition, done well, confirms the entire concept in practice.

### LLM Integration — Key Questions

- How is the behaviour config structured — what parameters exist, what are their ranges?
- How does LLM output map to config changes — direct JSON, or a translation layer?
- How is ambiguous or uninterpretable input handled — graceful failure or generative noise?
- What is the feedback loop — how does the player see the effect of their broadcast?
- How does the broadcast interface feel tonally — it should feel like a pulse, not a chat box

## 9. Open Mechanic Questions

*These remain unresolved and should be returned to as development progresses:*

- How and exactly when does the reframe land — what is the trigger moment?
- Who or what is issuing mission objectives to the player — and does the game address this fourth layer of control?
- How granular is the behaviour config the LLM modifies — and how visible are its effects?
- What is the tactile/input language of losing control of Litta — how does degradation feel at the controller?
- Are there multiple endings, or two broad paths with variations within them?
- How are rival Aksjub represented in the strategy layer — AI opponents, political entities, or both?
- Can the player bond with and puppet attendant cadre members directly, as with Litta?
- Does Litta's path have sub-choices — is there agency in how her recovery unfolds?
